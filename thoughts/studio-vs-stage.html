Studio > Stage

Over a long enough period of time, people will come to realize the value of presence as a competitive advantage. We're early.

Most of what passes for creative output right now is performance. The tweet crafted for engagement. The LinkedIn post reverse-engineered from what did well last week. The deck designed to impress before it's meant to inform. The stage has colonised the studio.

This is understandable. Visibility is a real input. Distribution matters. But there's a compounding problem: when you spend enough time performing, you stop having anything real to perform about. The work gets thin because the work is the performance.

The studio is different. The studio is where you work on things before they're ready to be seen. Where you follow threads because they're interesting, not because they're legible to an audience. Where you're allowed to be wrong, slow, and confused for long enough that something true can emerge.

Most people have optimised the stage so aggressively that the studio has nearly disappeared. Which means anyone who protects real studio time — unperformed thinking, genuine experimentation, work that doesn't need to be content — is compounding on something most people are actively depleting.

Presence as competitive advantage isn't about being offline. It's about having an inner life that your work can actually draw from. The stage is real. But without the studio, there's nothing to say.
