The Problem

Expanding Bridge Athletic's product into a new market segment.

Bridge Athletic has spent the past 10 years building a robust strength and conditioning platform used by elite sports teams — Chelsea Football Club, Manchester City, Golden State Warriors. They wanted to expand into personal training, and were looking to create a product roadmap for PTs.

Personal training is a crowded market. Once a tool gets adopted, it tends to stick — data lock-in makes switching painful regardless of quality.

To break in, a new product would have to be differentiated enough to pull coaches away from entrenched tools — or find a gap the incumbents hadn't addressed.

Scope

Product strategy, research, design

Domain

Fitness / health-tech SaaS

Duration

~6 weeks

Segmentation

Building for all = building for none.

Most competitors made the same mistake: they segmented by delivery method, profession, or geography — or not at all — and tried to serve everyone. Tools for online and in-person coaching. For trainers, nutritionists, gyms, and mental health professionals. Core coaching and tax automation. Every sport. Every market.

The result is bloated software that nobody loves but everyone is stuck in.

Bridge Acquire

Early-career trainers don't have a skills problem. They have a sales problem.

"Personal trainers would rather just train people. Selling themselves is a major issue and the reason some trainers quit."

— Secondary research

Recently certified trainers are early-stage solo entrepreneurs running out of runway. Institutions taught them anatomy and programming — not sales. The first 12 months are make or break.

They're encountering a trust and differentiation problem. A potential client scrolling Instagram has no way to tell a qualified, experienced trainer from someone who just looks fit. Most competitors in this space have built scheduling tools or marketplaces. None of them solved the trust problem at the moment it actually matters: before the first conversation.

Borrowed Trust Signal — Bridge Athletic is used by your favourite players at Golden State Warriors, Chelsea FC, Manchester City FC and more.

No other competitor can manufacture this trust signal.

I mapped the entire acquisition funnel — from the moment someone decides they need a trainer, through Instagram, WhatsApp, to the sale.

The critical moment: when a potential client clicks the link in a trainer's Instagram bio. Today they land on a Linktree, Calendly, or their own poorly-designed personal website. None communicate proof this trainer is worth the premium.

The decision

Obvious move

Build a marketplace. Match clients to trainers. Take a cut. But a marketplace solves the wrong problem — PTs don't lack reach. And it introduces a cold-start trap: neither side has reason to join until the other is already there.

What I proposed

Arm trainers with a credibility layer: verified certs, client outcomes, scheduling — where the sale already happens: on social and messaging channels.

Bridge Acquire gives certified PTs a professional profile — verified credentials, client reviews with outcomes, direct booking — accessible from their Instagram bio. Not a marketplace. A trust signal at the exact moment someone is deciding whether to reach out.

Verified credentials, experience, reviews. The proof that justifies the premium.

Bridge Engage

Established trainers don't need more features. They need to nourish the relationship that drives everything.

"Using this app requires MORE work than the workouts themselves! The platform is so bad that I have to quit her program."

— Client review, Everfit

"This is slowly making me hate my beloved trainer."

— Client review, Trainerize

This isn't a UX problem. It's a design philosophy problem. Every competitor built coach-first, feature-heavy platforms, then tacked on a client experience. The products bloated because the intention was the same across all of them.

When the intention is the same and no differentiating principles exist, you end up at the same solution. "Better UX" is not a strategy. A different design principle for a narrower audience — that's a strategy. And since competitors are architecturally committed to their current design, rebuilding from a different principle isn't a feature update — it's starting over. The gap compounds.

The ecosystem

Every revenue stream, every referral, every tool — all of it depends on one thing.

The relationship between trainer and client. Word of mouth is the primary growth engine. Referrals flow when clients experience genuine progress — not when the app is flashy. When the client feels cared for.

Build a product that nourishes that relationship and everything else follows. Build one that gets in the way and clients start blaming their trainer for the app.

Click a node to explore

The principle: the product exists to nourish the relationship.

Every design decision flows from one question — does this make the client feel more cared for and the trainer more effective at demonstrating progress?

Who wins if you win

Client feels cared for. The app opens to coach chat. Meals, workouts logged in conversation — creating a sense of sharing. Progress is visible, not buried in dashboards.

↓ therefore

Trainer grows revenue. Engaged clients stay longer, refer friends, buy supplements, opt into online training when travelling. Retention improves. Differentiation is built into the product.

↓ therefore

Bridge becomes irreplaceable. Not because of features. Because it's the layer where the relationship lives — the communication hub, the source of truth, the thing neither side wants to leave.

The product doesn't serve the trainer. It serves the relationship. The trainer's success is a byproduct.

Left: client view — chat-first, meals in conversation. Right: coach view — assistant coach surfaces insights. Never speaks to the client directly.

The AI assistant that never talks to the client.

The assistant coach — visible only to the trainer — surfaces insights, suggests messages, prepares pre-session briefs. It makes the coach more effective.

But it never speaks directly to the client.

The relationship between coach and client is what makes progress happen. AI as intermediary would dilute the very thing that makes the product work.

In a market where progress depends on one person feeling genuinely cared for by another, the role of technology is to make the caring more visible — not to automate it.

The Network

If Bridge Engage works, it unlocks something much larger.

Trainers' clients also work with nutritionists, physiotherapists, mental health professionals, doctors. Today, each works in their own silo.

If Bridge becomes the communication hub, it could expand into a market network — allied health professionals collaborating around a single client's health.

Non-competitive professionals collaborating around client health. Referrals flow freely because no one competes with each other.

Not a feature addition. A network effect. A fundamentally different competitive position from anything existing players could bolt on.
